A Field Notebook

Raising the Stone

No iron. No pulleys. No electricity. We set out to find out whether the physics of rope, sand, and human muscle could really explain the Great Pyramid — and built a simulator to test it, course by course, as we went.

Follow the reasoning
01

The question we actually started with

2.3 million blocks. 146.6 metres tall. Built by a civilization with no iron tools, no pulleys, and obviously no engines — just copper chisels, wooden sledges, rope, and an enormous number of people.

That's the part that's easy to find in any textbook. What's harder to find is whether the numbers actually work — whether you can take the real mass of a real block, the real friction of sand on stone, and the real pulling strength of one person, and watch a believable team move it up a believable ramp. So instead of reading another summary, we decided to do the arithmetic ourselves and see where it led.

We started as small as the problem allows: one block, one ramp, one team of haulers.

First model — single block, single ramp
02

Starting with one block

A core limestone block in the Great Pyramid weighs about 2.5 tonnes. To move it up a ramp, three forces matter: how much the block weighs, how much of that weight gravity uses to pull it back down the slope, and how much friction resists the sledge sliding across the surface beneath it. Add those together and you know exactly how much pulling force a team needs to supply.

That 0.30 friction coefficient isn't a guess — it's wetted sand specifically. A 2014 University of Amsterdam study (inspired by a tomb painting showing a worker pouring water just ahead of a sledge) found that wetting the sand in front of a sledge roughly halves the dragging force needed, by causing the sand grains to clump rather than pile up. Old Kingdom Egyptians appear to have known this empirically, four thousand years before anyone measured why it worked.

So: 44 people, one rope, a core block — entirely plausible. That answered the easy version of the question. Then we asked what happens at the other end of the size range, because the Great Pyramid isn't only built from 2.5-tonne blocks. The granite beams roofing the King's Chamber run up to 80 tonnes.

That contrast is the real finding from the single-block model: it's not that the pyramid is "impossible," it's that one method doesn't cover every block. The core mass of the pyramid is entirely explained by rope-and-ramp hauling. The largest granite pieces almost certainly needed something else layered in — most likely levers and wooden rocking cradles to walk them into final position, rather than a simple drag up an open ramp.

Second model — the whole build, course by course
03

What actually changes as it climbs

A single block on a single ramp tells you whether the physics works once. It doesn't tell you what happens on course 100 of 201, when the pyramid is already 90 metres tall and still climbing. So we built the full geometry: 201 courses, each one a shrinking square slab, each with its own block mass, its own ramp length, its own team size — and let the simulation walk all the way up.

Three things change as the structure rises, and they don't change at the same rate:

  • Block mass shrinks gently. Upper-course stones are smaller and lighter than the base — roughly 2.5 tonnes down to 1.2 tonnes by the top.
  • The team needed barely moves. Because mass tapers so gradually, the hauler count only drops from 44 to about 21 over the entire height.
  • The ramp explodes in length. To keep a constant, haulable angle, ramp length is simply height ÷ sin(angle) — and height keeps climbing while the angle can't. At the base the ramp is 8 metres. By the final course it's over a kilometre.
What this means

Labor was never the limiting factor in this model. The ramp's footprint was. By roughly a third of the way up, the ramp needed to reach the summit is already longer than the pyramid's own 230-metre base — which is exactly why Egyptologists don't think a single straight ramp reached the top. Some combination of steepening, spiraling around the faces, or shrinking ramp cross-section almost certainly took over partway through the build.

A gap in the evidence, honestly stated
05

So what happened to the ramp?

This is one of the genuinely open questions, not a solved one. We know the ramps weren't left standing — they were taken apart in stages as the building neared completion. Fragments of construction ramps survive at other pyramid sites (most notably the unfinished pyramid of Sekhemkhet), built from mudbrick, packed rubble, and limestone debris, and clearly meant to be temporary.

The outer casing stones — the smooth white limestone that once gave the pyramid its finished, polished face — appear to have been trimmed from the top down, as each level of ramp was cleared away to expose the surface beneath it. That sequencing makes sense: you can't dress and polish a face that's still buried under your own scaffolding.

The honest gap

No complete ramp from the Great Pyramid itself survives in place. What we have is ramp evidence from other, smaller or unfinished sites, plus tool marks, worker graffiti, and quarry trails — enough to constrain what's plausible, not enough to settle the straight-vs-spiral debate definitively. Our simulator picks one constant-angle model because it makes the math legible, not because it's the confirmed design.

06

Does this settle "humans, not aliens"?

It's worth being precise about what this kind of model actually proves, because it's easy to overstate.

What it does show: that rope, friction, and human muscle are physically sufficient to move every stone in the structure — including the 80-tonne granite beams, given enough haulers and ropes, or more likely levers for the largest pieces. That's a real, calculable result, and it's the same kind of calculation engineers and Egyptologists have run for decades, just made interactive here.

What it doesn't do: close the case on ramp design, which is still genuinely debated, or treat "ancient aliens" as a competing scientific hypothesis. It isn't one — there's no alien artifact, no inconsistency in the tool marks, no anomaly in the worker graffiti or the skeletal remains of the laborers (all of which exist, and all of which point to a large, organized human workforce). The physics was never really in question among researchers; this project just walks through why, with the actual numbers laid bare instead of asserted.

Third model — bottom-up timing, not top-down
07

How long does placing one block actually take?

Herodotus's account implies roughly twenty years of construction. Divide that by 2.3 million blocks and you get a tidy "one block every few minutes" — but that number is built top-down, working backward from a deadline. We wanted the bottom-up version: actually time one block's full cycle, using the same force model already built, and see if the two views agree.

One block's full cycle has three stages: haul it up the ramp, lever it into its final seated position against its neighbor, and walk the team back down for the next one.

The pattern is almost entirely explained by the walk, not the work. Levering a block into place takes a fixed five minutes regardless of height. But the haul up and the walk back both scale directly with ramp length — and since ramp length is the one quantity that grows without bound as the pyramid rises, cycle time grows with it. By the final courses, one team spends over an hour on a single block, more than ten times longer than at the base.

08

Closing the loop: why thousands of workers

If a single team's cycle time stretches to over an hour per block near the top, the only way the whole project finishes in twenty years is if many teams work in parallel — on different ramps, different faces, or staggered along one wide one. We summed the total hauling work required across all 201 courses and divided by the total working time available in twenty years at ten daylight hours a day.

The Scribe's Reckoning — Whole-Project Pace
Total hauling work requiredsum of every block's cycle time, across all 201 courses
≈ 3.8 billion person-seconds
Working time available20 years × 10 daylight hours × 3,600s
≈ 263 million seconds
Parallel teams neededwork required ÷ time available
~15 teams

Fifteen simultaneous hauling teams, each running 30–50 people, puts somewhere around 400–700 people actively hauling at any given moment — and that's only the haulers. It doesn't count the quarry workers cutting each block free, the masons dressing and fitting stone, the people feeding, housing, and organizing everyone else, or the engineers laying out each course's geometry. Workforce estimates from tomb and settlement evidence at Giza run to several thousand people total, in rotating gangs — which is exactly the kind of number this bottom-up math predicts you'd need, not a coincidence layered on top of it.

That's the real shape of the answer: not one impossibly fast team moving one block every few minutes for twenty years straight, but many ordinary teams, each working at a pace the physics fully supports, organized at a scale large enough to make the deadline. The pyramid isn't evidence of lost technology. It's evidence of logistics.
